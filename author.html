“
There are writers who arrive at their vocation young, certain of what they have to say. Peter Moroney arrived later, delayed by a life fully lived. Writing was always a passion, but it took a diagnosis of stage 4 cancer to make it urgent. With it came an altered perspective, and the hard-won knowledge of what it costs to look honestly at the past.

Background

Peter Moroney spent much of his working life in IT, finance, and consulting – practical, problem-solving work that kept him useful and occupied, driven and ambitious, but at a distance from the interior life that would eventually demand its due. Now in his sixties and based in Melbourne, he has turned that same analytical precision toward something far less certain: the excavation of memory, the archaeology of the self.

He came to serious writing not through a workshop or formal study, but through the need to make sense of experience, and to live fully within it. Live long enough and you begin to understand that certain things – that sense, that understanding – must be said before they are lost. His formation as a writer is entirely self-made, shaped by decades of reading, reflection, and the steady discipline of putting words on the page until they begin to mean something.

The Writing

Peter Moroney writes in two distinct but related modes. His fiction – three novels completed or in progress – explores how people live with the damage done to them: the long aftermath of harm, the private compromises we make to keep going, and how the past resurfaces in the body and the choices we make long after the original wound. These are not novels of resolution. They resist simplification and remain attentive to ambiguity.

Alongside the fiction runs an extensive body of autobiographical writing, centred on his long-running journals and blog Hieronymous. Here, Peter undertakes a sustained exploration of memory, returning to earlier periods of his life through diaries, fragments, and recollection. The work develops slowly, often without predetermined conclusions, characterised by intellectual honesty and emotional courage – a refusal to tidy experience into something more palatable than it was.

Across both forms, a central question persists: how do we carry what has been done to us? How does damage shape a life – and how, sometimes, does it also enlarge one?

Works

Send to Darkness
Send to Darkness follows disgraced ex-detective Tom Harbour, who is drawn into the disappearance of a shady petty criminal – a case that leads back to his dead brother, Ray, once a decorated officer and now a mythic figure in Melbourne’s criminal underworld. As Tom traces a network of blackmail built on hidden-camera recordings and political leverage, he is forced to confront the extent of Ray’s legacy and his own failure to see it for what it was. What begins as an investigation becomes a descent into corruption, memory, and self-deception, as the case closes in on those closest to him. With the truth threatening not only his survival but the woman he has come to love, Tom is driven toward a final reckoning in which loyalty, truth, and identity can no longer be separated.
Complete
Unknown to God
After years of withdrawal from public life, a former AFL player is drawn back into the world he abandoned when a former teammate dies under uncertain circumstances. As he revisits the past he has worked to contain, the boundaries between memory and present reality begin to shift. The novel moves between external investigation and internal reckoning, tracing how a life shaped by silence and avoidance begins to fracture under pressure, and how the past, once suppressed, returns with consequences that cannot be deferred.
Complete
The Shadow Light
A novel about lost authorship and the persistence of the past. Years after abandoning his early ambitions, a man discovers that a novel he wrote in youth has been published under his name, submitted without his knowledge by someone he once loved. As success arrives too late, he must reckon with the life he relinquished and the self he left behind.

Themes

Across his fiction and autobiographical writing, Peter Moroney returns to the forces that shape a life after damage: memory, guilt, silence, concealment, and the uneasy possibility of redemption.

  • The long aftermath of personal damage
  • Memory as both revelation and distortion
  • Identity shaped by concealment, loss, and abandonment
  • Guilt, masculinity, and moral responsibility
  • The tension between truth and self-preservation

Why Now

“I always wanted to write, from when I was a freckle-faced kid. The urge came naturally, and I returned to it over the years as a way of bringing order to what I felt, what I hoped for, and what I imagined. But I was too busy living to settle into it. Too much to see and do, so much to experience and understand, so much to conquer. I wanted to live big.

I did. I had my adventures. I had a full life. And then, without warning, I was hit by cancer. It was touch-and-go for a while, but I survived. I'm still the same person, but I see life differently, and my experience of it has changed.

I needed to put it into words. I needed to explore it properly. I needed to reach inside to understand what I was before I forgot altogether. So I finally did the thing I had always promised myself I would do – I began my first novel.

All those years when I would write without purpose returned to me, and I understood it was the apprenticeship for this moment, and that the rich life of mine was actually the necessary material for my writing. I wasn't delayed: the timing wasn't right.”
